Abstract 9

In noisy environments, visible speech articulations improve listening comprehension. The benefit derives from 10 

several sources, including articulatory timing and shape. Recent research has shown that visual cortex encodes a 11 

categorical representation of articulatory features and that visual speech can benefit both acoustic and phonetic 12 

feature processing separately. The present study advances the hypothesis that the shape of the articulators 13 

specifically influences the categorization of auditory speech in terms of its phonetic features. We tested this by 14 

linearly modeling electroencephalographic responses to natural, continuous speech (in noise) in terms of the 15 

acoustic and articulatory features of the speech. We compared the performance of these models in conditions 16 

where the speech was accompanied by a natural video of the speaker with their mouth visible, and a video 17 

where their mouth was covered by a dynamic ellipse obscuring articulatory shape but preserving dynamics. The 18 

dynamic mask reduced comprehension, neural processing of phonetic features, the associated multisensory 19 

benefits, and indices of visual-only linguistic processing over occipital scalp. Our findings support substantial 20 

visual involvement in speech comprehension, derived largely from the shape of the articulators. They also 21 

corroborate several proposals involving audiovisual speech processing hierarchy and the nature of the 22 

information contained in visible speech. 23 

Keywords 24 audiovisual speech | EEG | lipreading | linear modeling | visual 25 

Highlights 26 

 Visual speech provides at least two forms of information to enhance acoustic speech processing: 27 redundant temporal dynamics and complementary articulatory information 28 

 Covering the mouth with a dynamic mask preserves horizontal and vertical lip movement 29 information, but largely removes articulatory detail 30 

 Visual speech with a mask preserves some general multisensory benefits but removes visual 31 linguistic information and its ability to enhance auditory processing at the level of phonetic 32 features. 33

Background 1Listeners understand speech better when they can see the talker producing the utterance. 2This is especially true in challenging listening environments when speech acoustics are degraded by 3noise or masked by distracting speech [1,2]. Several properties of audiovisual speech have been 4highlighted to explain how visual speech improves audio speech comprehension: temporally 5correlated dynamics of audio and visual speech, visible pre-articulatory movements that are predictive 6of subsequent articulations, and movements and articulatory shapes that provide complementary 7information that compensates for what is lost when acoustic speech is masked by noise. These 8properties have been invoked when explaining audiovisual speech benefits as deriving from various 9perceptual and cognitive processes such as selective attention [3,4], prediction [5], perceptual filling-in 10 [6], and linguistic constraint [7,8]. 11 Auditory speech processing is accomplished via multiple stages of analysis [9,10] that occur 12 rapidly [11] and in parallel [12] during speech perception. This is often proposed along with another 13 hypothesis positing that each stage of processing feeds back on previous stages, providing the basis 14 for predicting upcoming inputs [13]. Visual speech processing is similarly hierarchical, with evidence 15 suggesting that processing of visual speech spans the same range of linguistic analysis as acoustic 16 speech [14]. Indeed, neurophysiological evidence has supported the presence of both energetic and 17 linguistic representations in the visual system [15–17] and their importance toward processing the 18 information content of speech [18,19, c.f. 20]. 19 The idea of hierarchical speech encoding within both the visual and auditory systems is 20 consistent with the above-mentioned proposals that suggest that different features of visual speech 21 can somewhat independently influences auditory speech processing. Specifically, it has been 22 suggested that 1) the correlated timing of low-level visual speech dynamics provides predictive 23 information about the timing of auditory speech, and 2) higher representations of the shape/form of 24

Supporting 1evidence for these separate modes has been provided – for example – by studies wherein the 2articulatory details of visual speech stimuli were removed while preserving their dynamics, which 3resulted in intermediate levels of audiovisual benefits [21,22]. Moreover, this visual-timing benefit is 4only present if the speech sounds are presented in low levels of background noise [23,24]. And we found preliminary 14 evidence that the strength of this multisensory enhancement was more pronounced at the level of 15 phonetic processing for speech in noise relative to speech in quiet, indicating that listeners rely more 16 on articulatory details from visual speech in challenging listening conditions. 17 In the current study we sought to better understand how such hierarchical audiovisual speech 18 integration might depend on articulatory information in visual speech and its representation within 19 the visual system. Specifically, we tested how removing articulatory shape from visual speech 20 influenced audiovisual speech comprehension and the neural activity related to different levels of 21 linguistic analysis. In doing so we hoped to link several threads of research related to visual speech 22 and audiovisual integration along the speech hierarchy. To investigate 13 the perceptual and neural effects related to having access to the fine visible detail of the articulators, 14 we added two conditions – visual and audiovisual – in which we imposed an elliptical mask over the 15 mouth area that followed the vertical and horizontal movements of the lips (Vm and AVm; Fig 1a, b), 16 preserving their dynamics. At 18 the end of each trial, they gave a subjective intelligibility rating corresponding to the percentage of 19 words they thought they heard. 20

Discussion 14 In the present study, we investigated how visible lip dynamics and articulatory detail 15 contribute to speech comprehension and the neural responses to visual and audiovisual speech in 16 noise. Previously work has shown 1) that visual speech dynamics and form have separable effects on 17 speech processing [22]; 2) that, much like acoustic speech, visible speech signals are processed at 18 distinct hierarchical levels [15,16] with comprehension being more tightly linked to higher-level 19 linguistic processing [19]; and 3) that visual speech impacts speech processing seemingly distinctly 20 across multiple levels [25]. In the current study we have woven these threads together by showing 21 that visual phonetic responses are driven largely by the form of the articulators, as previously 22 hypothesized, and that these responses are the major contributors to audiovisual integration at the 23 phonetic feature level leading to improvements in comprehension. A multitude of audiovisual interactions during speech perception 6Our findings support a multi-faceted and hierarchical audiovisual speech system. The 7presence or absence of visible articulatory detail did not appear to provide any unique multisensory 8benefits at level of acoustic process. However, this manipulation had a profound impact on 9multisensory integration at the level of phonetic feature processing. This dissociation was likely 10 pronounced, by design, due to our speech stimuli being presented in a high level of background noise 11 [25]. Indeed, with few specific exception [e.g., 6], the dynamics of the lips seem to have little potential 12 for enhancing speech comprehension in moderate to high levels of noise (also see below). Instead, the 13 spatial processing of vision is sensitive to subtle articulatory details that the auditory system struggles 14 with [e.g., place of articulation, 50]. According to our results, access to these visual form cues gives rise 15 to viseme responses in visual cortex and in-turn supplements and/or constrains auditory phonetic 16 feature responses, meaningfully toward improved comprehension in the listener. 17 The idea presented here of a multitude of audiovisual interactions aligns well with previous 18 neural findings. We and other groups have shown that the influence of visual speech varies across 19 linguistic level, goal, neural mechanism, visual feature, and time-scale [8,25,51–53]. This work also 20 aligns well with a strong base of behavioral experiments. One relatively consistent finding is that 21 altering spatial detail from visual speech results in appreciably lower levels of comprehension benefits 22 [21,22,54–56], a finding we replicate. What is interesting is that, like our findings, the literature is 23 equivocal on gains that can be attributed to visual temporal information – with some studies finding 24

Complementary visual speech constrains phonetic inference 1The above ideas are largely consistent with three proposals regarding vision’s effects on 2speech processing. In one proposal [70], Campbell divides visual speech cues into two types: 3

correlated features that provide redundant information to auditory signals as a backup, and 4

complementary features that contain information that, in the auditory modality, is not well resolved in 5the presence of noise (e.g., place of articulation). Separately, Peele and Sommers propose that vision 6provides prediction of upcoming speech articulations to the auditory system, and constraint regarding 7the linguistic inferences that are drawn from degraded speech. It’s intuitive to see how signals that are 8correlated have the ability to predict one another when one happens first, as vision mostly does [48], 9and how two complementary (i.e., non-overlapping) signals have the capacity for constraint [7]. 10 Finally, Kim and Davis [22] frame the visible portions of speech in terms of its timing or its cadence, 11 and its form of the articulators, or the spatial configuration and interaction of the tongue, teeth, and 12 lips. The analysis framework and resulting findings of the present study are largely consistent with all 13 three of these proposals. We show that temporal correlations from the masked speech provide only 14 modest behavioral benefits and only modest neurophysiological signatures of multisensory 15 integration. Meanwhile, we relate articulatory structure to the encoding of visemes, which implicitly 16 captures the complementary nature of visual speech, and also relate it to multisensory enhancement 17 of phonetic feature responses, which we contend is a consequence of linguistic constraint imposed by 18 vision. 19 Our results speak to the importance of fine spatial detail in visual speech and the 20 complementary information that it provides in challenging listening conditions. Although implicitly 21 we have coarsely dichotomized the visual speech concepts of timing/form and 22 redundant/complementary along a common divider, not all visual dynamics are redundant with the 23 acoustic dynamics.
