Abstract —In this paper, we investigate the tradeoffs between compute allocation and model performance for two speech-processing tasks: Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) and Speech Emotion Recognition (SER). We propose a unified frame-work that analyzes three fundamental compute dimensions: model size ( N ), input length ( T ), and representation resolu-tion (V ). Motivated by recent advances in compute optimal scaling for multimodal models, we systematically vary these dimensions to examine their influence on task performance under fixed computational budgets. Our study provides insights into how compute resources can be optimally distributed across model capacity, temporal context, and representational granularity, offering practical guidelines for the design of efficient speech models. Through experiments on LibriSpeech and CREMA-D datasets, we demonstrate non-linear scaling behavior and identify optimal operating points. Our results show that (1) increas-ing model size yields diminishing returns—scaling Tiny (39M) to Small (244M) reduces WER by 8.22%, whereas Small to Medium (769M) reduces WER by only 2.35%; (2) an optimal audio duration of approximately 4 seconds exists for SER; and (3) reducing encoder token resolution provides an effective mechanism for lowering inference cost, Large-v3 (1540M) with 750 frames requires 2572 GFLOPS whereas with 1500 frames requires 5228 GFLOPS, with less than 3% relative increase in WER. Additionally, LoRA-based adaptation enables efficient finetuning with minimal performance degradation. Code: https://github.com/vyomya/SAME

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Recent advances in large-scale audio models such as Whis-per [1], which demonstrates strong robustness across di-verse datasets, and wav2vec 2.0 [2], which enables power-ful self-supervised representation learning, have significantly improved performance in speech recognition and audio under-standing tasks [11]. However, these gains come with substan-tial computational costs, limiting their deployment in resource-constrained settings. Parameter-efficient finetuning methods such as LoRA [3] reduce the number of trainable parameters by introducing low-rank updates, enabling efficient adaptation with reduced compute. Scaling law studies suggest that performance improves predictably with increased compute; however, more recent work highlights that imbalanced allocation across compute dimensions leads to inefficiencies. In particular, Inference Compute-Optimal Video Vision Language Models [4] show that optimal inference performance depends on jointly scaling model capacity, input resolution, and token budget, rather than scaling any single axis in isolation. Our work extends this idea to the audio domain by defining analogous compute axes (xN , x T , x V ), studying their interac-tion in encoder decoder architectures, and empirically validat-ing compute-optimal tradeoffs for ASR and SER. Additionally, we incorporate insights from DAMA (Depth-Aware Model Adaptation) [5], which improves parameter-efficient finetuning by adapting layers selectively based on their importance, leading to better compute performance trade-offs compared to uniform adaptation strategies. This paper addresses the following question: How should computational resources be optimally allocated across model size xN , input length xT , and representation resolution xV to maximize performance under a fixed compute budget? 

Rather than uniformly scaling all components, we study how targeted allocation along different compute axes affects performance. We understand that optimal performance arises from balanced scaling across axes, rather than maximizing any single dimension [4]. Our contributions include:

A detailed analysis for optimizing compute performance tradeoffs in ASR and SER.  

> •

Systematic exploration of compute axes xN , xT , xV , with empirical evidence that their relative importance is task-dependent.

We investigate compute performance tradeoffs across three complementary axes—model size xN , input length xT , and en-coder token resolution xV . These axes form a structured search space where xN governs representational capacity but offers the least flexibility post-deployment; xT provides a dynamic, input-dependent compute handle tunable without architectural changes; and xV reduces cross-attention cost orthogonally to both other axes. Critically, these axes interact non-linearly: gains from additional temporal context are amplified in larger models, while resolution subsampling becomes increasingly effective as encoder sequences grow. Jointly varying all three under a fixed FLOPs budget reveals Pareto-optimal configura-tions invisible to any single-axis analysis, providing principled guidance for deploying audio models under real-world com-pute constraints. Critically, we find that the relative priority of these axes differs qualitatively between tasks: for ASR all three axes are active and the Pareto frontier is smooth, while for SER the resolution axis is inapplicable and the frontier is sparse, confirming that compute axis priorities cannot be assumed to transfer across tasks without empirical validation. 

A. ASR Pipeline (Whisper) 

We finetune Whisper models on LibriSpeech using parameter-efficient adaptation, leveraging all three compute axes to characterize the full compute performance tradeoff space for speech recognition. 1) Model Size xN : We evaluate Whisper-Tiny, Small, Medium, and Large-v3, spanning 39M to 1540M pa-rameters. 2) Input Length xT : We control input length via ac-tive encoder frames: Full (1500 frames) and Reduced (750 frames). 3) Resolution xV : We subsample encoder outputs before cross-attention using a stride factor xV = 1 or xV = 2 ,reducing key/value tokens passed to the decoder by ∼O(Tdec × Tenc ).In the ASR setting, all three axes are fully applicable. Com-pute is best allocated by selecting a model size appropriate to the deployment budget, maximizing input length for temporal context, and applying resolution subsampling to reduce cross-attention cost, a priority ordering empirically validated by our Pareto frontier analysis. 

B. SER Pipeline (wav2vec2) 

We finetune wav2vec2-large-robust on CREMA-D for 6-class emotion classification [9]. Unlike ASR, the SER pipeline surfaces important task-specific constraints that alter how each axis operates; however, xV is treated as fixed for architectural reasons described below. 1) Model Size xN : We evaluate wav2vec2-base and wav2vec2-large-robust, spanning 95M to 317M param-eters. 2) Input Length xT : We evaluate audio durations of 2s, 4s, and 6s. Unlike ASR where additional context is gener-ally beneficial, SER exhibits a non-trivial optimum, too-short clips truncate prosodic patterns while excessively long clips introduce speaker-neutral content and padding artifacts. 3) Resolution xV : No explicit subsampling is applied. SER relies on pooled frame-level representations where token reduction is less architecturally natural, and the dominant compute cost lies in the transformer encoder rather than cross-attention. We treat xV as fixed and identify it as a direction for future work. The SER pipeline therefore operates primarily over xN

and xT , revealing a qualitatively different scaling behavior from ASR. The Pareto frontier is sparse rather than smooth, suggesting task complexity saturates at the capacity level of wav2vec2-base and that compute is better allocated toward adaptation strategy than model scale. 

C. Optimization Strategies 

Full finetuning of large pretrained audio models is com-putationally prohibitive and risks catastrophic like forgetting of pretrained acoustic representations. We therefore adopt two complementary parameter-efficient strategies that together address both the breadth and depth of adaptation, and sweep their key hyperparameters to characterize their individual contributions to the compute performance tradeoff. 

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation): LoRA constrains weight updates to a low-rank factorization, preserving pretrained attention structure while dramatically reducing trainable pa-rameters. This is well suited to audio models where pretrained representations should be retained rather than overwritten. We sweep rank r ∈ { 8, 16 , 32 , 64 } to characterize the tradeoff between adaptation capacity and parameter overhead, with higher ranks enabling richer task-specific updates at increasing compute cost. 

DAMA (Depth-Aware Model Adaptation): Uniform adap-tation ignores the established asymmetry between transformer layers—lower layers encode general acoustic features while upper layers capture abstract, task-specific representations. DAMA exploits this by selectively unfreezing top encoder layers, concentrating adaptation capacity where it is most impactful. This is especially critical for SER, where emotion-discriminative features emerge primarily in upper layers and LoRA alone proves insufficient. We sweep unfrozen top en-coder layers ∈ { 0, 4, 8} to identify the minimum adaptation depth required for effective domain transfer, directly quanti-fying the compute cost of task-specific layer unfreezing. IV. E XPERIMENTAL SETUP 

A. Datasets 

To systematically evaluate compute performance tradeoffs across audio understanding tasks, we require benchmarks that are well-established, diverse in their acoustic demands, and widely used in prior work, enabling direct comparison against published baselines. We therefore select one dataset per task, chosen to reflect complementary challenges in temporal modeling and emotional discriminability. 

ASR-LibriSpeech: To evaluate ASR performance under controlled compute budgets, we benchmark against Lib-riSpeech [6], the standard reference dataset for English speech recognition. Following prior work on Whisper [1] and ef-ficient ASR adaptation, we train on train-clean-100 

(100 hours) to reflect a realistic mid-scale supervised setting, validate on the standard validation set, and report final results on test-other , the more acoustically challenging split

SER-CREMA-D: We use CREMA-D [7], a controlled, balanced corpus of 7,442 clips from 91 actors expressing six discrete emotions, which has served as a standard benchmark in recent emotion recognition studies. Compared to in-the-wild corpora, CREMA-D’s controlled recording conditions isolate the effect of compute axis choices from confounding acous-tic variability, making it well-suited for systematic scaling analysis. We apply an 80/10/10 train/val/test split based on speaker identity to prevent speaker leakage, a common source of inflated results in prior SER work, ensuring that reported accuracies reflect generalization to unseen speakers rather than memorization of speaker-specific vocal characteristics. 

B. Model Variants 

To characterize the xN axis across a meaningful range of model capacities, we select model variants that span one to two orders of magnitude in parameter count, following the scaling convention established in the original Whisper [1] and wav2vec 2.0 [2] papers. Rather than evaluating every available checkpoint, we select variants at approximately uniform log-scale intervals to expose diminishing returns a pattern con-sistently observed in NLP and speech scaling studies while remaining computationally tractable under our fixed FLOPs budget constraint. 

Whisper variants (ASR xN sweep): We evaluate Whisper-Tiny (39M), Small (244M), Medium (769M), and Large-v3 (1540M), covering a ∼40 × range in parameter count. Prior work has demonstrated that Whisper-Large achieves near state-of-the-art WER on standard benchmarks, while Tiny and Small operate in the efficiency regime targeted by on-device deployment. We apply LoRA to all variants to isolate the effect of model capacity from the cost of full finetuning, with LoRA parameter counts ranging from ∼0.5M (Tiny) to ∼24.6M (Large-v3), remaining a small fraction of total parameters in all cases. 

Wav2vec2 variants (SER xN sweep): We evaluate wav2vec2-base (95M, 12 layers) and wav2vec2-large-robust (317M, 24 layers). The large-robust variant is pre-trained on diverse and noisy speech conditions [12], making it par-ticularly well-suited for emotion recognition where prosodic variation and recording quality directly affect model perfor-mance. The ∼3.3 × parameter ratio between base and large variants, combined with fixed LoRA rank ( r = 16 ), allows us to attribute performance differences to model capacity rather than adaptation budget. C. Training Configuration 

To ensure that observed performance differences reflect the effect of compute axis choices rather than optimization artifacts, we maintain a unified training configuration across all experiments. All models are trained using the AdamW optimizer with a linear learning rate schedule, a learning rate of 1 × 10 −5, 500 warmup steps, and 4000 total training steps, a budget sufficient for convergence on both LibriSpeech and CREMA-D at the scales studied, while remaining comparable to the finetuning protocols reported in prior LoRA-based speech adaptation work. LoRA for the Whisper ASR model is applied to qproj and vproj across all attention layers with rank r = 32 , α = 64 , and dropout = 0 .05 , following the configuration shown to generalize well across modalities [3]. Training is conducted with gradient checkpointing enabled to reduce memory overhead, allowing larger model variants to be finetuned within the same hardware budget without architectural compromise. B. ASR Results on LibriSpeech 

In contrast to the sparse SER Pareto frontier, ASR exhibits a smooth, monotonically improving frontier across all three. WER decreases monotonically with model size from 19.01% (Tiny, 29.3G FLOPs) to 4.85% (Large-v3, 5228G FLOPs); however, gains diminish sharply at scale as represented in Table V. The Tiny to Small transition yields the largest single improvement ( ∼8% absolute), while both Small to Medium and Medium to Large-v3 each contribute only ∼8% WER improvement at roughly 2× the FLOPs. Increasing input length from 750 to 1500 frames consistently reduces WER by 3–10% relative across all model sizes. Notably, the gain from doubling input length on Whisper-Small ( 10 .84% → 7.96% ,+370G FLOPs) is comparable in absolute WER improvement to upgrading Small to Medium, at less than one-sixth the additional compute. Stride-2 resolution subsampling (2 stride) achieves near identical accuracy to full resolution at roughly half the cross-attention FLOPs; for example, Small (1500 frames, 2 stride) reaches 8.26% WER at 510G FLOPs versus 7.96% at 722G FLOPs for full resolution, a 29%FLOPs saving for only 0.3% absolute WER degradation. VI. C ONCLUSION 

This work presents a systematic study of compute-performance tradeoffs in large pre-trained audio models across Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) and Speech Emotion Recognition (SER). By jointly varying three compute axes -model size ( xN ), input length ( xT ), and encoder token resolu-tion ( xV ), alongside parameter-efficient adaptation strategies (LoRA and DAMA), we identify optimal Pareto configurations with direct deployment implications.

In conclusion, our central finding is that balanced and targeted allocation of compute across multiple inference axes consistently outperforms strategies that maximize any sin-gle dimension in isolation. For automatic speech recognition (ASR), encoder token subsampling along the representation axis ( xV ) emerges as the most efficient inference-time lever, recovering approximately 29% of cross-attention FLOPs while incurring negligible degradation in accuracy. In addition, in-creasing input length ( xT ) provides a more compute-efficient performance gain than scaling model size ( xN ) in the mid-capacity regime; for example, doubling the context window in Whisper-Small achieves performance comparable to a full model upgrade while requiring less than one-sixth of the additional compute. For speech emotion recognition (SER), we observe a task-specific optimal operating point at approximately four seconds of audio. Shorter input durations result in loss of critical prosodic context, whereas longer durations introduce padding-induced artifacts that degrade performance. Collectively, these results highlight the importance of jointly optimizing compute allocation across model size, temporal context, and represen-tation resolution rather than relying on single-axis scaling strategies. Adaptation strategy proves equally critical. LoRA alone collapses SER performance to near random, while depth-aware layer unfreezing (DAMA) recovers meaningful accu-racy at identical FLOPs, demonstrating that tasks involving significant domain shift require structural adaptation, not just parameter-efficient overlays [8]. The optimal compute SER configuration achieves a 4.3× FLOPs reduction with only 7.7% accuracy degradation, underscoring that efficiency and performance are not fundamentally at odds. Notably, the Pareto frontier topology differs qualitatively between tasks smooth and monotonic for ASR, sparse for SER confirming that compute axis priorities are dependent on task and cannot be transferred without empirical validation. The three-axis framework ( xN ,xT ,xV ) introduced here provides a principled scaffold for compute budget allocation across audio tasks. VII. F UTURE WORK 

Joint axis optimization: Our current study uses astar-sweep strategy, varying one axis at a time. Fu-ture work should conduct iso-FLOP sweeps jointly over 

(xN , x T , x V , LoRA rank ) to identify the globally optimal con-figuration at each compute budget, analogous to the approach in [4]. 

Quantization as a compute axis: Post-training quantization (INT8, INT4) is orthogonal to the axes studied here and could unlock additional compute–performance tradeoffs [10]. Com-bining LoRA with quantization (e.g., QLoRA) is a particularly promising direction. 

Continuous xV subsampling in SER: Extending the en-coder token subsampling mechanism from the ASR pipeline to wav2vec2-based SER could expose additional compute savings without parameter overhead, completing the three-axis framework for both tasks. ACKNOWLEDGMENT
